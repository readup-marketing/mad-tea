Mystery at Blackrock Lighthouse

Ages 7-11

⏱️ 5 min read

📖 Reading Timer (Optional)
00:00

Finn pressed his nose against the car window as his dad's old estate car bumped along the narrow coastal road. Through the rain-streaked glass, he could see it in the distance—Blackrock Lighthouse, standing tall and lonely on its rocky island, connected to the mainland by a single narrow causeway.

"There it is!" Finn shouted, pointing excitedly.

His dad smiled. "That's going to be our home for the next two weeks while I repair the light mechanism. Pretty impressive, isn't it?"

Finn nodded enthusiastically. At ten years old, this was the most exciting thing that had ever happened to him. His dad was a lighthouse engineer, and usually Finn had to stay with his gran during the school holidays while his dad travelled for work. But this time, because it was such a remote location, his dad had been allowed to bring him along.

"We need to cross the causeway before high tide," his dad said, checking his watch. "Once the tide comes in, that road will be completely underwater for twelve hours."

As they drove across the narrow strip of road—barely wide enough for their car—Finn looked down at the dark water on either side. Waves crashed against the rocks, sending spray high into the air. It was both thrilling and slightly terrifying.

The lighthouse loomed larger as they approached. Up close, it was even more impressive—a towering structure of white stone with red stripes spiralling up its sides. At the base was a small cottage where the old lighthouse keepers used to live.

"Grab your bag," his dad said as they parked. "Let's get inside before this rain gets worse."

The cottage was cold and smelled faintly of salt and old wood. Finn explored while his dad started unpacking. There were three small bedrooms, a kitchen with an ancient stove, and a living room with a fireplace and walls covered in old photographs of lighthouse keepers from decades past.

"This place has been automated for twenty years," his dad explained, lighting the fire. "But the mechanism needs a major overhaul, so they've asked me to come and fix it. Should take about ten days."

That evening, after a dinner of soup and sandwiches, Finn's dad climbed the 127 steps to the lamp room at the top of the lighthouse to begin his inspection. Finn stayed in the cottage, reading a book by the fire, but he kept getting distracted by the sound of the wind howling outside and the waves crashing against the rocks.

Around nine o'clock, his dad returned looking puzzled.

"That's odd," he muttered, pulling off his work gloves.

"What's wrong?" Finn asked.

"Someone's been up there recently. Very recently. There are fresh footprints in the dust, and some of my tools have been moved."

"But nobody else is here," Finn said. "Right?"

His dad frowned. "Nobody should be here. The lighthouse is officially closed, and we're the only ones with permission to be on the island."

A loud crash from somewhere outside made them both jump. His dad grabbed a torch and headed for the door. "Stay here," he commanded.

But Finn followed anyway, curiosity overcoming caution. The beam of the torch swept across the courtyard outside the cottage. At first, they saw nothing but wind-blown rain. Then the light caught something that made them both freeze.

The door to the lighthouse tower—which his dad had definitely locked—was standing wide open, swinging in the wind.

🔧 Today's Activity

⏱️ Time: 15-20 minutes

🏗️ Engineer Your Lighthouse

The Challenge: Finn's dad is a lighthouse engineer who repairs and maintains lighthouses. Your job is to build a model lighthouse that can actually light up!

📦 What You'll Need:

Basic materials:

  • Empty cardboard tube (kitchen roll, wrapping paper tube, or Pringles can)
  • White paper or paint
  • Red paper, marker, or paint (for the stripes)
  • Small torch or LED tea light
  • Scissors and glue/tape
  • Cardboard for the base

Optional extras:

  • Tin foil (for a reflective lamp room)
  • Clear plastic cup (for the lamp room dome)
  • Black card (for windows)
  • Small rocks or playdough (to create the rocky island base)

🔨 Instructions:

1. Create the lighthouse tower:

  • Cover your cardboard tube with white paper
  • Add red spiral stripes like Blackrock Lighthouse
  • Cut out small black windows going up the tower

2. Build the lamp room at the top:

  • Cut a circle of cardboard slightly larger than your tube
  • Make small cuts around the edge and fold up to create a rim
  • Line the inside with tin foil to reflect light
  • Place your torch or LED light inside
  • If you have a clear plastic cup, cut it to fit as a dome on top

3. Create the base:

  • Cut a large circle of cardboard for the island
  • Glue your lighthouse tube to the center
  • Add grey/brown paint or paper for rocks
  • Optional: use playdough or stones to make it 3D

4. Add details:

  • Draw or create a small cottage at the base
  • Make a thin strip of card for the causeway connecting to "mainland"
  • Add blue paper or paint around the island for water

5. Test your light:

  • Turn on your torch/LED light in the lamp room
  • Does it shine through the top?
  • Take it into a dark room - how far can you see the light?

🤔 Engineering Challenge Questions:

After building, discuss:

  • Why do you think lighthouses are so tall?
    Think about: How far the light needs to shine, ships seeing it from a distance
  • Why is Blackrock Lighthouse striped?
    Think about: Ships needing to identify which lighthouse they're seeing during the day
  • What makes a good lighthouse location?
    Think about: Rocky coasts, dangerous areas for ships, visibility
  • Why did they paint lighthouses different patterns?
    Research: Each lighthouse has a unique "daymark" so sailors can identify it

📸 Photo Challenge:

Once you've built your lighthouse:

  • Take a photo of it in daylight
  • Take a photo of it in a dark room with the light on
  • Compare the two photos - which shows the lighthouse's purpose better?

💡 Did You Know?

  • The tallest lighthouse in the world is Jeddah Light in Saudi Arabia (133 meters tall!)
  • The oldest working lighthouse is the Tower of Hercules in Spain (built by the Romans around 100 AD!)
  • Before electricity, lighthouses used oil lamps and huge mirrors to magnify the light
  • Lighthouse keepers had to wind up clockwork mechanisms that rotated the light - just like Finn's dad is repairing!
